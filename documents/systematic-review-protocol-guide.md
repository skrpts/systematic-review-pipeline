## Guide to Writing a Systematic Review Protocol

### Why Write a Protocol?

A systematic review protocol is a detailed plan written before the review begins. It specifies:
- What the review will address (research questions)
- How it will find studies (search strategy)
- How it will select studies (inclusion/exclusion criteria)
- How it will extract and synthesise data
- How it will assess the quality of evidence

Protocols serve three essential purposes:

1. **Transparency** — they allow others to assess whether the review was conducted as planned, reducing the risk of selective reporting
2. **Reproducibility** — they provide sufficient detail for another team to replicate the review
3. **Rigour** — the act of writing a protocol forces you to think through methodological decisions before they are influenced by the results

### Protocol Structure

#### 1. Title
Follow PRISMA-P guidelines: include "systematic review" (and "meta-analysis" if applicable) and a structured description of the topic.

Example: "The effectiveness of mindfulness-based interventions for reducing anxiety in university students: a systematic review and meta-analysis"

#### 2. Registration
State where the protocol is registered and provide the registration number (or "to be registered").

#### 3. Authors and Contributions
List all review team members with their roles (lead reviewer, second screener, methodologist, information specialist, domain expert, statistician).

#### 4. Background
- Summarise the existing evidence on the topic (2-4 paragraphs)
- Identify the gap this review will address
- Explain why a systematic review is the appropriate approach
- Note any existing reviews and how yours differs

#### 5. Objectives
State the review objectives clearly. Use the structured research question developed using PICO, SPIDER, or equivalent framework.

Primary objective: "To assess the effectiveness of [intervention] for [outcome] in [population]"

Secondary objectives (if applicable): "To examine whether effectiveness varies by [subgroup characteristic]"

#### 6. Eligibility Criteria

**Inclusion criteria:**
- Population: [precise definition]
- Intervention/Exposure: [precise definition]
- Comparator: [precise definition]
- Outcome(s): [primary and secondary, with measurement definitions]
- Study design: [eligible designs]
- Date range: [with justification for any limits]
- Language: [with justification for any restrictions]
- Publication type: [peer-reviewed articles, grey literature, conference abstracts]

**Exclusion criteria:**
- [List each exclusion with justification]

#### 7. Information Sources
List all databases to be searched, including:
- Database name and platform (e.g., "MEDLINE via Ovid")
- Grey literature sources (theses repositories, trial registries, organisational websites)
- Supplementary search methods (citation chaining, hand-searching key journals, contacting experts)

#### 8. Search Strategy
Include the full search strategy for at least one database. State that strategies for other databases are available in the appendix or supplementary materials.

Note: The search strategy should be developed in consultation with an information specialist (librarian) where possible.

#### 9. Study Selection
Describe the screening process:
- Software used for screening (Covidence, Rayyan, EPPI-Reviewer)
- Deduplication method
- Number of reviewers for title/abstract screening
- Number of reviewers for full-text screening
- Process for resolving disagreements
- Plan for recording exclusion reasons at each stage

#### 10. Data Extraction
Describe:
- What data will be extracted (list all variables)
- Data extraction form (include as appendix or reference)
- Number of extractors and independence
- Process for handling missing data (contacting authors)
- Piloting procedure for the extraction form

#### 11. Risk of Bias Assessment
Specify:
- Tool(s) to be used (RoB 2, ROBINS-I, NOS, JBI, CASP)
- Domains to be assessed
- Number of assessors and independence
- How risk of bias results will be incorporated into the synthesis

#### 12. Synthesis
Describe the planned synthesis approach:

**Narrative synthesis:**
- How studies will be grouped for synthesis
- How patterns and relationships will be explored
- How robustness of the synthesis will be assessed

**Meta-analysis (if planned):**
- Effect measure(s): risk ratio, odds ratio, mean difference, standardised mean difference
- Model: fixed-effect or random-effects (with justification)
- Heterogeneity assessment: I-squared, Q statistic, prediction intervals
- Subgroup analyses (pre-specified)
- Sensitivity analyses (pre-specified)
- Meta-regression (if planned)
- Publication bias assessment: funnel plot, Egger's test (if >= 10 studies)

#### 13. Certainty of Evidence
Specify the framework to be used (typically GRADE for intervention reviews) and the outcomes for which certainty will be assessed.

#### 14. Timeline
Provide a realistic timeline:
- Protocol development and registration: 2-4 weeks
- Search execution: 1-2 weeks
- Screening: 2-6 weeks (depending on volume)
- Data extraction: 2-4 weeks
- Quality assessment: 1-2 weeks
- Synthesis and writing: 4-8 weeks

Total: 3-6 months is typical for a well-resourced team.

#### 15. Amendments
State that any amendments to the protocol after registration will be documented with date, description, and rationale.

### Common Protocol Mistakes

1. **Vague eligibility criteria** — "studies about mindfulness" is not precise enough. Specify the type of mindfulness intervention, the population, and the outcomes.
2. **No pre-specified synthesis plan** — deciding how to synthesise after seeing the data introduces bias.
3. **Overlooking grey literature** — systematic reviews should search beyond peer-reviewed databases.
4. **Single-reviewer screening** — this introduces an unacceptable risk of missed studies. Use at least two independent reviewers.
5. **Not piloting the extraction form** — forms that seem clear on paper often reveal ambiguities when applied to real studies.
6. **No plan for protocol amendments** — changes will happen. The protocol should state how they will be documented.
7. **Unrealistic timeline** — systematic reviews take 3-12 months. If your timeline is shorter, reconsider whether a systematic review is feasible or whether a rapid review would be more appropriate.
